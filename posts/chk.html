Chesapeake Energy's Governance Woes

(2012)

All eyes were on Oklahoma City this proxy season as Chesapeake Energy shareholders rumbled into the 2012 annual meeting to voice their discontent with the embattled natural gas giant. Perhaps the most contentious meeting of the season, certain key elements were in place for a memorable media bonanza – a lurking Carl Icahn, angry shareholders, government investigations, and a CEO characterized as a risk-addicted natural gas cowboy pulling the strings of a puppet board.

Chesapeake has never been acorporate governance sweetheart;as discussed in our prior ProxyPapers the Company has repeatedlyfailed to align executive pay withperformance, ignored majoritysupported shareholder proposals,and until recently, maintained aclassified board structure subject toa plurality voting standard. however,it was following two Reutersarticles investigating the tangledrelationship between the Companyand its CEo, aubrey mcClendon,that Chesapeake’s governance shortcomings werefully thrust into the public eye. The articles detailedhow mr. mcClendon had used his personal stake inCompany wells as collateral to take out as much as $1.1 billion in personal loans frominstitutions that simultaneouslyprovided financing to the Company.The articles also revealed mr.mcClendon’s prior involvement ina $200 million commodities hedgefund which took positions in the samegoods produced by the Company.

Following the Reuters articlesthe Company’s credit ratingwas downgraded by both S&Pand moody’s, which, along withcorporate governance concerns,cited the Company’s cash shortfallsas a reason for the downgrade – in2012 the Company took out a $4billion term loan in order to coverdebt obligations. The Company hasannounced asset sales totaling $4.7billion in the first half of 2012 andaims to raise an additional $8.3 billion from varioussales by the end of the year. In addition, the IRS andSEC opened investigations into mr. mcClendon’spersonal borrowings and certain large public pension funds initiated vote-no campaigns against the Company’s directors.

ahead of the annual meetingthe Company disclosedthat mr. mcClendon wouldbe stepping down aschairman of the board whileretaining his position asCEo. The Company alsodisclosed that it would bereducing the overall levelof compensation paid to itsdirectors and eliminating aperquisite providing for theirpersonal use of corporateaircraft. In addition, theCompany agreed to putforth a managementproposal providing for a majority vote standard indirector elections (a shareholder proposal requestingas much was originally approved at the Company’s2009 annual meeting) and disclosed that it wouldseek exemption from an oklahoma statute requiringa classified board structure for public companiesincorporated in that state. The Company helpedestablish the law in 2010 following majority approvedshareholder proposals seeking board declassificationat the 2008 and 2009 annual meetings. The Companyalso reached an agreementwith its two largestshareholders, Southeasternasset management and CarlIcahn (at the time, ownersof 13.6% and 7.6% of theCompany’s common stock,respectively) to reconstitutethe board by replacingfour current directors withthree nominees proposedby Southeastern and oneproposed by mr. Icahn.

Unfortunately for the board, their efforts proved to betoo little too late. approximately 424 million shares,or 66% of shares entitled to vote, were represented byproxy at the meeting (not including broker non-votes).both of the Company’s two director nominees weresoundly rejected, with Richard davidson receivingabout 27% of the votes cast and v. burns hargis approximately 26%. TheCompany’s advisory voteon executive compensationwas rejected with only20% support, as was theCompany’s annual IncentivePlan (which permits tax-deductions for performancebased awards granted tonEos) with only 31.3%approval. The managementproposal providing for amajority vote standard wasalso approved.

Three of the four shareholderproposals submitted atthe meeting were alsoapproved by shareholders:a request that the Companyre-incorporate to delaware, an elimination of certainsupermajority vote requirements, and a proxy accessproposal. Chesapeake is one of just two companieswhere proxy access was approved by shareholdersduring the 2012 proxy season (the other being nabors Industries). The non-binding proposal permits largeshareholders to nominate board candidates on the Company's ballot.

In June the Company released a filing announcingthat the board had accepted the resignation of mr. davidson while rejecting the resignation of mr.hargis. mr. hargis is currently the audit committeechairman and in charge of leading the Company’sinternal investigation into mr. mcClendon’s personalborrowings. mr. hargis is also President of oklahomaState University, which received at least $2.0 millionin commitments and donations from Chesapeake in 2011. The filing also stated that mr. hargis is notexpected to remain chairmanof the audit committee andthat upon completion of theinvestigation his resignation willbe reconsidered. Chesapeakehasn’t said when the board willfinish the internal inquiry.
